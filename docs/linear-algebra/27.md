# § 27. Inner Products and Norms

We assume that all vector spaces are over the field $F$, where $F$ denotes either $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$.

## Inner Product

!!! definition "Definition 27.1 : Inner Product"
    Let $V$ be a vector space over $F$.
    An **inner product** on $V$ is a function that assigns, to every ordered pair of vectors $x$ and $y$ in $V$, a scalar in $F$, denoted $\langle x, y\rangle$, such that for all $x, y$, and $z$ in $V$ and all $c$ in $F$, the following hold.

    - (a) $\langle x+z, y\rangle=\langle x, y\rangle+\langle z, y\rangle$.
    - (b) $\langle c x, y\rangle=c\langle x, y\rangle$.
    - (c) $\overline{\langle x, y\rangle}=\langle y, x\rangle$, where the bar denotes complex conjugation.
    - (d) $\langle x, x\rangle>0$ if $x \neq 0$.

    Note that (c) reduces to $\langle x, y\rangle=\langle y, x\rangle$ if $F=\mathbb{R}$.

!!! corollary "Corollary 27.2 : Linearity in the first component"
    Conditions (a) and (b) simply require that the inner product be linear in the first component.
    It is easily shown that if $a_{1}, a_{2}, \ldots, a_{n} \in F$ and $y, v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots, v_{n} \in V$, then

    $$
    \left\langle\sum_{i=1}^{n} a_{i} v_{i}, y\right\rangle=\sum_{i=1}^{n} a_{i}\left\langle v_{i}, y\right\rangle .
    $$

!!! definition "Definition 27.3 : Standard Inner Product on $F^{n}$"
    For $x=\left(a_{1}, a_{2}, \ldots, a_{n}\right)$ and $y=\left(b_{1}, b_{2}, \ldots, b_{n}\right)$ in $F^{n}$, define

    $$
    \langle x, y\rangle=\sum_{i=1}^{n} a_{i} \overline{b_{i}} .
    $$

    !!! proof
        We verify that $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ satisfies conditions (a) through (d) of **Definition 27.1**.

        Let $x=(a_{1},\ldots,a_{n})$, $y=(b_{1},\ldots,b_{n})$, and $z=(c_{1},\ldots,c_{n})$ be vectors in $F^{n}$, and let $c\in F$.

        - (a)  

            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            \langle x+z, y\rangle
            &=\sum_{i=1}^{n}(a_i+c_i)\overline{b_i}
            =\sum_{i=1}^{n}a_i\overline{b_i}+\sum_{i=1}^{n}c_i\overline{b_i}
            =\langle x, y\rangle+\langle z, y\rangle .
            \end{aligned}
            $$

        - (b)  

            $$
            \langle c x, y\rangle
            =\sum_{i=1}^{n}(c a_i)\overline{b_i}
            =c\sum_{i=1}^{n}a_i\overline{b_i}
            =c\langle x, y\rangle .
            $$

        - (c)  

            $$
            \overline{\langle x, y\rangle}
            =\overline{\sum_{i=1}^{n}a_i\overline{b_i}}
            =\sum_{i=1}^{n}\overline{a_i\overline{b_i}}
            =\sum_{i=1}^{n}\overline{a_i}\,b_i
            =\sum_{i=1}^{n}b_i\overline{a_i}
            =\langle y, x\rangle .
            $$

        - (d)

            $$
            \langle x, x\rangle=\sum_{i=1}^{n}a_i\overline{a_i}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}|a_i|^{2}\ge 0.
            $$

            If $x\neq 0$, then $a_j\neq 0$ for some $j$, so $|a_j|^2>0$, and therefore $\langle x,x\rangle>0$.


    This inner product is called the **standard inner product on $F^{n}$**.

    When $F=\mathbb{R}$ the conjugations are not needed, and in early courses this standard inner product is usually called the **dot product** and is denoted by $x \cdot y$ instead of $\langle x, y\rangle$.

!!! example "Example 27.4 : Inner product on $\mathcal{C}([0,1])$"
    Let $V=\mathcal{C}([0,1])$, the vector space of real-valued continuous functions on $[0,1]$.
    For $f, g \in V$, define $\langle f, g\rangle=\int_{0}^{1} f(t) g(t) d t$.
    Since the preceding integral is linear in $f$, (a) and (b) are immediate, and (c) is trivial.
    If $f \neq 0$, then $f^{2}$ is bounded away from zero on some subinterval of $[0,1]$ (continuity is used here), and hence $\langle f, f\rangle=\int_{0}^{1}[f(t)]^{2} d t>0$.

!!! definition "Definition 27.5 : Conjugate Transpose (Adjoint)"
    Let $A \in \mathrm{M}_{m \times n}(F)$.
    We define the **conjugate transpose** or **adjoint** of $A$ to be the $n \times m$ matrix $A^{*}$ such that $\left(A^{*}\right)_{i j}=\overline{A_{j i}}$ for all $i, j$.

!!! definition "Definition 27.6 : Frobenius Inner Product"
    Let $V=\mathrm{M}_{n \times n}(F)$, and define $\langle A, B\rangle=\operatorname{tr}\left(B^{*} A\right)$ for $A, B \in V$.
    (Recall that the trace of a matrix $A$ is defined by $\operatorname{tr}(A)=\sum_{i=1}^{n} A_{i i}$.)

    !!! proof
        We verify that $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ satisfies conditions (a) through (d) of **Definition 27.1**.

        - (a)  
            Let $A,B,C \in V$.
            Using $C^{*}(A+B)=C^{*}A+C^{*}B$ and $\operatorname{tr}(X+Y)=\operatorname{tr}(X)+\operatorname{tr}(Y)$, we have

            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            \langle A+B, C\rangle
            & =\operatorname{tr}\left(C^{*}(A+B)\right)
            =\operatorname{tr}\left(C^{*}A+C^{*}B\right) \\
            & =\operatorname{tr}\left(C^{*}A\right)+\operatorname{tr}\left(C^{*}B\right)
            =\langle A, C\rangle+\langle B, C\rangle .
            \end{aligned}
            $$

        - (b)  
            Let $c \in F$ and $A,B \in V$.
            Since $B^{*}(cA)=c(B^{*}A)$ and $\operatorname{tr}(cX)=c\,\operatorname{tr}(X)$, we obtain
            
            $$
            \langle cA, B\rangle
            =\operatorname{tr}\left(B^{*}(cA)\right)
            =\operatorname{tr}\left(c\,B^{*}A\right)
            =c\,\operatorname{tr}\left(B^{*}A\right)
            =c\langle A,B\rangle .
            $$

        - (c)  
            Let $A,B \in V$.
            First note that
            
            $$
            \overline{\operatorname{tr}(X)}=\operatorname{tr}(X^{*})
            $$

            for any square matrix $X$, because $\overline{\sum_i X_{ii}}=\sum_i \overline{X_{ii}}=\sum_i (X^{*})_{ii}$.
            Hence, using $(B^{*}A)^{*}=A^{*}B$ and $\operatorname{tr}(Y)=\operatorname{tr}(Y^{t})$, we have
            
            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            \overline{\langle A,B\rangle}
            & =\overline{\operatorname{tr}(B^{*}A)}
            =\operatorname{tr}\left((B^{*}A)^{*}\right)
            =\operatorname{tr}(A^{*}B)
            =\langle B,A\rangle .
            \end{aligned}
            $$

        - (d)  
            Let $A \in V$.
            We compute
            
            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            \langle A,A\rangle
            & =\operatorname{tr}(A^{*}A)
            =\sum_{i=1}^{n}(A^{*}A)_{ii}
            =\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n}(A^{*})_{ik}A_{ki} \\
            & =\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\overline{A_{ki}}\,A_{ki}
            =\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n}|A_{ki}|^{2}.
            \end{aligned}
            $$

            If $A\neq O$, then $A_{ki}\neq 0$ for some $k,i$, so at least one term $|A_{ki}|^{2}$ is positive, and therefore $\langle A,A\rangle>0$.

    This inner product on $\mathrm{M}_{n \times n}(F)$ is called the **Frobenius inner product**.

!!! definition "Definition 27.7 : Inner Product Space"
    A vector space $V$ over $F$ endowed with a specific inner product is called an **inner product space**.
    If $F=\mathbb{C}$, we call $V$ a **complex inner product space**, whereas if $F=\mathbb{R}$, we call $V$ a **real inner product space**.

!!! concept "Concept 27.8 : Integration of complex-valued functions"
    At this point, we mention a few facts about integration of complex-valued functions.
    First, the imaginary number $i$ can be treated as a constant under the integration sign.
    Second, every complex-valued function $f$ may be written as $f=f_{1}+i f_{2}$, where $f_{1}$ and $f_{2}$ are real-valued functions.
    Thus we have

    $$
    \int f=\int f_{1}+i \int f_{2} \quad \text { and } \quad \overline{\int f}=\int \overline{f}
    $$

!!! example "Example 27.9 : Inner product on $H$"
    A very important inner product space that resembles $\mathcal{C}([0,1])$ is the space $H$ of continuous complex-valued functions defined on the interval $[0,2 \pi]$ with the inner product

    $$
    \langle f, g\rangle=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{0}^{2 \pi} f(t) \overline{g(t)} d t
    $$

    The reason for the constant $1 / 2 \pi$ will become evident later.
    This inner product space, which arises often in the context of physical situations, is examined more closely in later sections.

    !!! proof
        We verify that $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ satisfies conditions (a) through (d) of **Definition 27.1**.

        - (a)  
            Let $f,g,h \in H$.
            Using linearity of the integral and the identity $(f+h)\overline{g}=f\overline{g}+h\overline{g}$, we have

            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            \langle f+h, g\rangle
            & =\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi}(f(t)+h(t))\overline{g(t)}\,dt \\
            & =\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi}f(t)\overline{g(t)}\,dt
              +\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi}h(t)\overline{g(t)}\,dt \\
            & =\langle f,g\rangle+\langle h,g\rangle .
            \end{aligned}
            $$

        - (b)  
            Let $c \in \mathbb{C}$ and $f,g \in H$.
            Since $(cf)\overline{g}=c(f\overline{g})$ and the integral is homogeneous, we obtain

            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            \langle cf, g\rangle
            & =\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi}(cf(t))\overline{g(t)}\,dt
            =c\cdot \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi}f(t)\overline{g(t)}\,dt \\
            & =c\,\langle f,g\rangle .
            \end{aligned}
            $$

        - (c)  
            Let $f,g \in H$.
            Using $\overline{\int \phi}=\int \overline{\phi}$ and $\overline{f\overline{g}}=\overline{f}\,g$, we have

            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            \overline{\langle f,g\rangle}
            & =\overline{\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi} f(t)\overline{g(t)}\,dt}
            =\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi}\overline{f(t)\overline{g(t)}}\,dt \\
            & =\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi}\overline{f(t)}\,g(t)\,dt
            =\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi} g(t)\overline{f(t)}\,dt
            =\langle g,f\rangle .
            \end{aligned}
            $$

        - (d)  
            Let $f \in H$.
            Then
            
            $$
            \langle f,f\rangle
            =\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi} f(t)\overline{f(t)}\,dt
            =\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi} |f(t)|^{2}\,dt .
            $$

            Since $|f(t)|^{2}\ge 0$ for all $t$, we have $\langle f,f\rangle\ge 0$.
            If $f\neq 0$, then there exists $t_{0}\in[0,2\pi]$ with $f(t_{0})\neq 0$.
            By continuity, $|f(t)|^{2}>0$ on some interval around $t_{0}$, so the integral is strictly positive.
            Hence $\langle f,f\rangle>0$ when $f\neq 0$.

        Therefore $\langle f,g\rangle=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi} f(t)\overline{g(t)}\,dt$ defines an inner product on $H$.

!!! theorem "Theorem 27.10 : Basic properties of inner products"
    Let $V$ be an inner product space.
    Then for $x, y, z \in V$ and $c \in F$, the following statements are true.

    - (a) $\langle x, y+z\rangle=\langle x, y\rangle+\langle x, z\rangle$.
    - (b) $\langle x, c y\rangle=\overline{c}\langle x, y\rangle$.
    - (c) $\langle x, 0\rangle=\langle 0, x\rangle=0$.
    - (d) $\langle x, x\rangle=0$ if and only if $x=0$.
    - (e) If $\langle x, y\rangle=\langle x, z\rangle$ for all $x \in V$, then $y=z$.

    !!! proof
        - (a)  
            We have

            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            \langle x, y+z\rangle
            & =\overline{\langle y+z, x\rangle}
            =\overline{\langle y, x\rangle+\langle z, x\rangle} \\
            & =\overline{\langle y, x\rangle}+\overline{\langle z, x\rangle}
            =\langle x, y\rangle+\langle x, z\rangle .
            \end{aligned}
            $$

        - (b)  
            Using conjugate symmetry and linearity in the first variable,

            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            \langle x, c y\rangle
            & =\overline{\langle c y, x\rangle}
            =\overline{c\langle y, x\rangle}
            =\overline{c}\,\overline{\langle y, x\rangle}
            =\overline{c}\,\langle x, y\rangle .
            \end{aligned}
            $$

        - (c)  
            Since $0=0x$ for any scalar $0\in F$, by linearity in the first variable we have $\langle 0, x\rangle=\langle 0x, x\rangle=0\langle x,x\rangle=0$.
            Then by conjugate symmetry,

            $$
            \langle x, 0\rangle=\overline{\langle 0, x\rangle}=\overline{0}=0 .
            $$

        - (d)  
            If $x=0$, then $\langle x,x\rangle=\langle 0,0\rangle=0$ by (c).

            Conversely, if $\langle x,x\rangle=0$, then $x$ cannot be nonzero, since the positivity axiom of an inner product says that $\langle x,x\rangle>0$ whenever $x\neq 0$.
            Hence $x=0$.

        - (e)  
            Assume that $\langle x, y\rangle=\langle x, z\rangle$ for all $x\in V$.
            Then for all $x\in V$,

            $$
            \langle x, y-z\rangle=\langle x,y\rangle-\langle x,z\rangle=0,
            $$

            using part (a) and the fact that $y+(-z)=y-z$.
            Taking $x=y-z$ gives $\langle y-z, y-z\rangle=0$.
            By part (d), this implies $y-z=0$, and hence $y=z$.

!!! corollary "Corollary 27.11 : Conjugate linearity in the second component"
    The reader should observe that (a) and (b) of **Theorem 27.10** show that the inner product is conjugate linear in the second component.

## Norm

!!! definition "Definition 27.12 : Norm"
    Let $V$ be an inner product space.
    For $x \in V$, we define the **norm** or **length** of $x$ by $\|x\|=\sqrt{\langle x, x\rangle}$.

!!! example "Example 27.13 : Euclidean norm on $F^{n}$"
    Let $V=F^{n}$.
    If $x=\left(a_{1}, a_{2}, \ldots, a_{n}\right)$, then

    $$
    \|x\|=\left\|\left(a_{1}, a_{2}, \ldots, a_{n}\right)\right\|=\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left|a_{i}\right|^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}
    $$

    is the Euclidean definition of length.
    Note that if $n=1$, we have $\|a\|=|a|$.

!!! theorem "Theorem 27.14 : Cauchy-Schwarz and triangle inequalities"
    Let $V$ be an inner product space over $F$.
    Then for all $x, y \in V$ and $c \in F$, the following statements are true.

    - (a) $\|c x\|=|c| \cdot\|x\|$.
    - (b) $\|x\|=0$ if and only if $x=0$.
        In any case, $\|x\| \geq 0$.
    - (c) (Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality) $|\langle x, y\rangle| \leq\|x\| \cdot\|y\|$.
    - (d) (Triangle Inequality) $\|x+y\| \leq\|x\|+\|y\|$.

    !!! proof
        - (a)  
            By definition, $\|x\|=\sqrt{\langle x,x\rangle}$.
            Using linearity in the first variable and conjugate symmetry,

            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            \|cx\|^{2}
            & =\langle cx, cx\rangle
            =c\langle x, cx\rangle
            =c\,\overline{\langle cx, x\rangle}
            =c\,\overline{c\langle x,x\rangle} \\
            & =c\,\overline{c}\,\overline{\langle x,x\rangle}
            =|c|^{2}\langle x,x\rangle
            =|c|^{2}\|x\|^{2}.
            \end{aligned}
            $$

            Taking square roots (and noting both sides are $\ge 0$) gives $\|cx\|=|c|\,\|x\|$.

        - (b)  
            Since $\|x\|=\sqrt{\langle x,x\rangle}$ and $\langle x,x\rangle\ge 0$ for all $x$, we have $\|x\|\ge 0$.

            If $x=0$, then $\|x\|^{2}=\langle 0,0\rangle=0$, so $\|x\|=0$.

            Conversely, if $\|x\|=0$, then $\|x\|^{2}=\langle x,x\rangle=0$.
            By positive definiteness of the inner product, this implies $x=0$.

        - (c)  
            If $y=0$, then $|\langle x,y\rangle|=0\le \|x\|\|y\|$.
            Assume $y\neq 0$.
            For any scalar $c\in F$,

            $$
            0\le \|x-cy\|^{2}=\langle x-cy,\,x-cy\rangle.
            $$

            Expand using linearity in the first variable, part (b) of **Theorem 27.10**, and conjugate symmetry:

            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            \langle x-cy,\,x-cy\rangle
            & =\langle x, x-cy\rangle-\langle cy, x-cy\rangle \\
            & =\langle x,x\rangle-\overline{c}\langle x,y\rangle-c\langle y,x\rangle+|c|^{2}\langle y,y\rangle.
            \end{aligned}
            $$

            Choose

            $$
            c=\frac{\langle x,y\rangle}{\langle y,y\rangle},
            $$

            which is valid because $\langle y,y\rangle>0$ when $y\neq 0$.
            Then $\overline{c}=\dfrac{\langle y,x\rangle}{\langle y,y\rangle}$ and $|c|^{2}=\dfrac{|\langle x,y\rangle|^{2}}{\langle y,y\rangle^{2}}$.
            Substituting gives

            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            0
            & \le \langle x,x\rangle
            -\frac{|\langle x,y\rangle|^{2}}{\langle y,y\rangle}
            -\frac{|\langle x,y\rangle|^{2}}{\langle y,y\rangle}
            +\frac{|\langle x,y\rangle|^{2}}{\langle y,y\rangle} \\
            & =\langle x,x\rangle-\frac{|\langle x,y\rangle|^{2}}{\langle y,y\rangle}.
            \end{aligned}
            $$

            Hence

            $$
            |\langle x,y\rangle|^{2}\le \langle x,x\rangle\,\langle y,y\rangle=\|x\|^{2}\|y\|^{2},
            $$

            and taking square roots yields $|\langle x,y\rangle|\le \|x\|\,\|y\|$.

        - (d)  
            Compute
            
            $$
            \begin{aligned}
            \|x+y\|^{2}
            & =\langle x+y, x+y\rangle \\
            & =\langle x,x\rangle+\langle x,y\rangle+\langle y,x\rangle+\langle y,y\rangle \\
            & =\|x\|^{2}+\|y\|^{2}+\langle x,y\rangle+\overline{\langle x,y\rangle}.
            \end{aligned}
            $$

            Since $\langle x,y\rangle+\overline{\langle x,y\rangle}=2\Re\langle x,y\rangle$ and $\Re\langle x,y\rangle\le |\langle x,y\rangle|$, we have

            $$
            \|x+y\|^{2}\le \|x\|^{2}+2|\langle x,y\rangle|+\|y\|^{2}.
            $$

            By (c), $|\langle x,y\rangle|\le \|x\|\,\|y\|$, so

            $$
            \|x+y\|^{2}\le \|x\|^{2}+2\|x\|\,\|y\|+\|y\|^{2}=(\|x\|+\|y\|)^{2}.
            $$

            Taking square roots (both sides are $\ge 0$) gives $\|x+y\|\le \|x\|+\|y\|$.

!!! example "Example 27.15 : Cauchy-Schwarz and triangle inequalities in $F^{n}$"
    For $F^{n}$, we may apply (c) and (d) of **Theorem 27.14** to the standard inner product to obtain the following well-known inequalities:

    $$
    \left|\sum_{i=1}^{n} a_{i} \overline{b_{i}}\right| \leq\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left|a_{i}\right|^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left|b_{i}\right|^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}
    $$

    and

    $$
    \left[\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left|a_{i}+b_{i}\right|^{2}\right]^{1 / 2} \leq\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left|a_{i}\right|^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}+\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left|b_{i}\right|^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}
    $$

## Orthogonality and Orthonormality

!!! definition "Definition 27.16 : Orthogonality and Orthonormality"
    Let $V$ be an inner product space.
    Vectors $x$ and $y$ in $V$ are **orthogonal** (**perpendicular**) if $\langle x, y\rangle=0$.
    A subset $S$ of $V$ is **orthogonal** if any two distinct vectors in $S$ are orthogonal.
    A vector $x$ in $V$ is a unit vector if $\|x\|=1$.

    Finally, a subset $S$ of $V$ is **orthonormal** if $S$ is orthogonal and consists entirely of unit vectors.
    Note that if $S=\left\{v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots\right\}$, then $S$ is orthonormal if and only if $\left\langle v_{i}, v_{j}\right\rangle=\delta_{i j}$, where $\delta_{i j}$ denotes the Kronecker delta.

!!! definition "Definition 27.17 : Normalizing"
    The process of multiplying a nonzero vector by the reciprocal of its length is called **normalizing**.

!!! example "Example 27.18 : An orthonormal subset in $H$"
    Recall the inner product space $H$.
    We introduce an important orthonormal subset $S$ of $H$.
    For any integer $n$, let $f_{n}(t)=e^{i n t}$, where $0 \leq t \leq 2 \pi$.
    (Recall that $e^{i n t}=\cos n t+i \sin n t$.)
    Now define $S=\left\{f_{n}: n \text { is an integer }\right\}$.
    Clearly $S$ is a subset of $H$.
    Using the property that $\overline{e^{i t}}=e^{-i t}$ for every real number $t$, we have, for $m \neq n$,

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \left\langle f_{m}, f_{n}\right\rangle & =\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{0}^{2 \pi} e^{i m t} \overline{e^{i n t}} d t=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{0}^{2 \pi} e^{i(m-n) t} d t \\
    & =\left.\frac{1}{2 \pi i(m-n)} e^{i(m-n) t}\right|_{0} ^{2 \pi}=0
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    Also,

    $$
    \left\langle f_{n}, f_{n}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{0}^{2 \pi} e^{i(n-n) t} d t=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{0}^{2 \pi} 1 d t=1
    $$

    In other words, $\left\langle f_{m}, f_{n}\right\rangle=\delta_{m n}$.

!!! exercise "Exercise 27.22"
    Let $V$ be a real or complex vector space (possibly infinite-dimensional), and let $\beta$ be a basis for $V$.

!!! definition "Definition 27.19 : Norm"
    Let $V$ be a vector space over $F$, where $F$ is either $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$.
    Regardless of whether $V$ is or is not an inner product space, we may still define a **norm** $\|\cdot\|$ as a real-valued function on $V$ satisfying the following three conditions for all $x, y \in V$ and $a \in F$.

    - (1) $\|x\| \geq 0$, and $\|x\|=0$ if and only if $x=0$.
    - (2) $\|a x\|=|a| \cdot\|x\|$.
    - (3) $\|x+y\| \leq\|x\|+\|y\|$.

!!! exercise "Exercise 27.26"
    Let $\|\cdot\|$ be a norm on a vector space $V$, and define, for each ordered pair of vectors, the scalar $d(x, y)=\|x-y\|$, called the distance between $x$ and $y$.
    Prove the following results for all $x, y, z \in V$.
    
    - (a) $d(x, y) \geq 0$.
    - (b) $d(x, y)=d(y, x)$.
    - (c) $d(x, y) \leq d(x, z)+d(z, y)$.
    - (d) $d(x, x)=0$.
    - (e) $d(x, y) \neq 0$ if $x \neq y$.

!!! exercise "Exercise 27.27"
    Let $\|\cdot\|$ be a norm on a real vector space $V$ satisfying the parallelogram law given in **Exercise 27.11**.
    Define

    $$
    \langle x, y\rangle=\frac{1}{4}\left[\|x+y\|^{2}-\|x-y\|^{2}\right] .
    $$

    Prove that $\langle\cdot, \cdot\rangle$ defines an inner product on $V$ such that $\|x\|^{2}=\langle x, x\rangle$ for all $x \in V$.
    
    Hints:
    
    - (a) Prove $\langle x, 2 y\rangle=2\langle x, y\rangle$ for all $x, y \in V$.
    - (b) Prove $\langle x+u, y\rangle=\langle x, y\rangle+\langle u, y\rangle$ for all $x, u, y \in V$.
    - (c) Prove $\langle n x, y\rangle=n\langle x, y\rangle$ for every positive integer $n$ and every $x, y \in V$.
    - (d) Prove $m\left\langle\frac{1}{m} x, y\right\rangle=\langle x, y\rangle$ for every positive integer $m$ and every $x, y \in V$.
    - (e) Prove $\langle r x, y\rangle=r\langle x, y\rangle$ for every rational number $r$ and every $x, y \in V$.
    - (f) Prove $|\langle x, y\rangle| \leq\|x\| \|y\|$ for every $x, y \in V$.
        
        Hint: Condition (3) in the definition of norm can be helpful.

        $$

        $$

    - (h) Use the fact that for any $c \in \mathbb{R}$, $|c-r|$ can be made arbitrarily small, where $r$ varies over the set of rational numbers, to establish item (b) of the definition of inner product.
