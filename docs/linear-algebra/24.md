# § 24. Diagonalizability

## Algebraic Multiplicity and Dimension of Eigenspace

!!! theorem "Theorem 24.1 : Distinct eigenvalues give linearly independent eigenvectors."
    Let $T$ be a linear operator on a vector space $V$, and let $\lambda_{1}, \lambda_{2}, \ldots, \lambda_{k}$ be distinct eigenvalues of $T$.
    If $v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots, v_{k}$ are eigenvectors of $T$ such that $\lambda_{i}$ corresponds to $v_{i}$ $(1 \leq i \leq k)$, then $\left\{v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots, v_{k}\right\}$ is linearly independent.

    !!! proof
        The proof is by mathematical induction on $k$.
        Suppose that $k=1$.
        Then $v_{1} \neq 0$ since $v_{1}$ is an eigenvector, and hence $\left\{v_{1}\right\}$ is linearly independent.
        Now assume that the theorem holds for $k-1$ distinct eigenvalues, where $k-1 \geq 1$, and that we have $k$ eigenvectors $v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots, v_{k}$ corresponding to the distinct eigenvalues $\lambda_{1}, \lambda_{2}, \ldots, \lambda_{k}$.
        We wish to show that $\left\{v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots, v_{k}\right\}$ is linearly independent.
        Suppose that $a_{1}, a_{2}, \ldots, a_{k}$ are scalars such that

        $$
        a_{1} v_{1}+a_{2} v_{2}+\cdots+a_{k} v_{k}=0
        $$

        Applying $T-\lambda_{k} I$ to both sides of (1), we obtain

        $$
        a_{1}\left(\lambda_{1}-\lambda_{k}\right) v_{1}+a_{2}\left(\lambda_{2}-\lambda_{k}\right) v_{2}+\cdots+a_{k-1}\left(\lambda_{k-1}-\lambda_{k}\right) v_{k-1}=0
        $$

        By the induction hypothesis $\left\{v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots, v_{k-1}\right\}$ is linearly independent, and hence

        $$
        a_{1}\left(\lambda_{1}-\lambda_{k}\right)=a_{2}\left(\lambda_{2}-\lambda_{k}\right)=\cdots=a_{k-1}\left(\lambda_{k-1}-\lambda_{k}\right)=0 .
        $$

        Since $\lambda_{1}, \lambda_{2}, \ldots, \lambda_{k}$ are distinct, it follows that $\lambda_{i}-\lambda_{k} \neq 0$ for $1 \leq i \leq k-1$.
        So $a_{1}=a_{2}=\cdots=a_{k-1}=0$, and (1) therefore reduces to $a_{k} v_{k}=0$.
        But $v_{k} \neq 0$ and therefore $a_{k}=0$.
        Consequently $a_{1}=a_{2}=\cdots=a_{k}=0$, and it follows that $\left\{v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots, v_{k}\right\}$ is linearly independent.

!!! corollary "Corollary 24.2 : $n$ distinct eigenvalues imply diagonalizability."
    Let $T$ be a linear operator on an $n$-dimensional vector space $V$.
    If $T$ has $n$ distinct eigenvalues, then $T$ is diagonalizable.

    !!! proof
        Suppose that $T$ has $n$ distinct eigenvalues $\lambda_{1}, \ldots, \lambda_{n}$.
        For each $i$ choose an eigenvector $v_{i}$ corresponding to $\lambda_{i}$.
        By **Theorem 24.1**, $\left\{v_{1}, \ldots, v_{n}\right\}$ is linearly independent, and since $\operatorname{dim}(V)=n$, this set is a basis for $V$.
        Thus, by **Theorem 23.3**, $T$ is diagonalizable.

The converse of **Theorem 24.1** is false.
That is, it is not true that if $T$ is diagonalizable, then it has $n$ distinct eigenvalues.

!!! definition "Definition 24.3 : Polynomial Splitting over a Field"
    A polynomial $f(t)$ in $\mathrm{P}(F)$ **splits over $F$** if there are scalars $c, a_{1}, \ldots, a_{n}$ (not necessarily distinct) in $F$ such that

    $$
    f(t)=c\left(t-a_{1}\right)\left(t-a_{2}\right) \cdots\left(t-a_{n}\right)
    $$

!!! theorem "Theorem 24.4 : Characteristic polynomial of a diagonalizable operator splits."
    The characteristic polynomial of any diagonalizable linear operator splits.

    !!! proof
        Let $T$ be a diagonalizable linear operator on the $n$-dimensional vector space $V$, and let $\beta$ be an ordered basis for $V$ such that $[T]_{\beta}=D$ is a diagonal matrix.
        Suppose that

        $$
        D=\left(\begin{array}{cccc}
        \lambda_{1} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
        0 & \lambda_{2} & \cdots & 0 \\
        \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\
        0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda_{n}
        \end{array}\right)
        $$

        and let $f(t)$ be the characteristic polynomial of $T$.
        Then

        $$
        \begin{aligned}
        f(t) & =\operatorname{det}(D-t I)=\operatorname{det}\left(\begin{array}{cccc}
        \lambda_{1}-t & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
        0 & \lambda_{2}-t & \cdots & 0 \\
        \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\
        0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda_{n}-t
        \end{array}\right) \\
        & =\left(\lambda_{1}-t\right)\left(\lambda_{2}-t\right) \cdots\left(\lambda_{n}-t\right)=(-1)^{n}\left(t-\lambda_{1}\right)\left(t-\lambda_{2}\right) \cdots\left(t-\lambda_{n}\right) .
        \end{aligned}
        $$

The converse of **Theorem 24.4** is false; that is, the characteristic polynomial of $T$ may split, but $T$ need not be diagonalizable.

!!! definition "Definition 24.5 : Algebraic Multiplicity of an Eigenvalue"
    Let $\lambda$ be an eigenvalue of a linear operator or matrix with characteristic polynomial $f(t)$.
    The **(algebraic) multiplicity** of $\lambda$ is the largest positive integer $k$ for which $(t-\lambda)^{k}$ is a factor of $f(t)$.

!!! definition "Definition 24.6 : Eigenspace"
    Let $T$ be a linear operator on a vector space $V$, and let $\lambda$ be an eigenvalue of $T$.
    Define $E_{\lambda}=\{x \in V: T(x)=\lambda x\}=\mathrm{N}\left(T-\lambda I_{V}\right)$.
    The set $E_{\lambda}$ is called the **eigenspace** of $T$ corresponding to the eigenvalue $\lambda$.

    Analogously, we define the **eigenspace** of a square matrix $A$ to be the eigenspace of $L_{A}$.

!!! theorem "Theorem 24.7 : Eigenspace dimension is at most its multiplicity."
    Let $T$ be a linear operator on a finite-dimensional vector space $V$, and let $\lambda$ be an eigenvalue of $T$ having multiplicity $m$.
    Then $1 \leq \operatorname{dim}\left(E_{\lambda}\right) \leq m$.

    !!! proof
        Choose an ordered basis $\left\{v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots, v_{p}\right\}$ for $E_{\lambda}$, extend it to an ordered basis $\beta=\left\{v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots, v_{p}, v_{p+1}, \ldots, v_{n}\right\}$ for $V$, and let $A=[T]_{\beta}$.
        Observe that $v_{i}$ $(1 \leq i \leq p)$ is an eigenvector of $T$ corresponding to $\lambda$, and therefore

        $$
        A=\left(\begin{array}{cc}
        \lambda I_{p} & B \\
        O & C
        \end{array}\right)
        $$

        By **Exercise 21.21**, the characteristic polynomial of $T$ is

        $$
        \begin{aligned}
        f(t) & =\operatorname{det}\left(A-t I_{n}\right)=\operatorname{det}\left(\begin{array}{cc}
        (\lambda-t) I_{p} & B \\
        O & C-t I_{n-p}
        \end{array}\right) \\
        & =\operatorname{det}\left((\lambda-t) I_{p}\right) \operatorname{det}\left(C-t I_{n-p}\right) \\
        & =(\lambda-t)^{p} g(t)
        \end{aligned}
        $$

        where $g(t)$ is a polynomial.
        Thus $(\lambda-t)^{p}$ is a factor of $f(t)$, and hence the multiplicity of $\lambda$ is at least $p$.
        But $\operatorname{dim}\left(E_{\lambda}\right)=p$, and so $\operatorname{dim}\left(E_{\lambda}\right) \leq m$.

!!! theorem "Lemma 24.8"
    Let $T$ be a linear operator, and let $\lambda_{1}, \lambda_{2}, \ldots, \lambda_{k}$ be distinct eigenvalues of $T$.
    For each $i=1,2, \ldots, k$, let $v_{i} \in E_{\lambda_{i}}$, the eigenspace corresponding to $\lambda_{i}$.
    If

    $$
    v_{1}+v_{2}+\cdots+v_{k}=0
    $$

    then $v_{i}=0$ for all $i$.

    !!! proof
        Suppose otherwise.
        By renumbering if necessary, suppose that, for $1 \leq m \leq k$, we have $v_{i} \neq 0$ for $1 \leq i \leq m$, and $v_{i}=0$ for $i>m$.
        Then, for each $i \leq m$, $v_{i}$ is an eigenvector of $T$ corresponding to $\lambda_{i}$ and

        $$
        v_{1}+v_{2}+\cdots+v_{m}=0
        $$

        But this contradicts **Theorem 24.1**, which states that these $v_{i}$ s are linearly independent.
        We conclude, therefore, that $v_{i}=0$ for all $i$.

!!! theorem "Theorem 24.9 : Union of linearly independent eigenspace subsets is independent."
    Let $T$ be a linear operator on a vector space $V$, and let $\lambda_{1}, \lambda_{2}, \ldots, \lambda_{k}$ be distinct eigenvalues of $T$.
    For each $i=1,2, \ldots, k$, let $S_{i}$ be a finite linearly independent subset of the eigenspace $E_{\lambda_{i}}$.
    Then $S=S_{1} \cup S_{2} \cup \cdots \cup S_{k}$ is a linearly independent subset of $V$.

    !!! proof
        Suppose that for each $i$

        $$
        S_{i}=\left\{v_{i 1}, v_{i 2}, \ldots, v_{i n_{i}}\right\}
        $$

        Then $S=\left\{v_{i j}: 1 \leq j \leq n_{i}\right.$, and $\left.1 \leq i \leq k\right\}$.
        Consider any scalars $\left\{a_{i j}\right\}$ such that

        $$
        \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sum_{j=1}^{n_{i}} a_{i j} v_{i j}=0
        $$

        For each $i$, let

        $$
        w_{i}=\sum_{j=1}^{n_{i}} a_{i j} v_{i j} .
        $$

        Then $w_{i} \in E_{\lambda_{i}}$ for each $i$, and $w_{1}+\cdots+w_{k}=0$.
        Therefore, by **Lemma 24.8**, $w_{i}=0$ for all $i$.
        But each $S_{i}$ is linearly independent, and hence $a_{i j}=0$ for all $j$.
        We conclude that $S$ is linearly independent.

!!! theorem "Theorem 24.10 : Diagonalizability is characterized by eigenspace dimensions."
    Let $T$ be a linear operator on a finite-dimensional vector space $V$ such that the characteristic polynomial of $T$ splits.
    Let $\lambda_{1}, \lambda_{2}, \ldots, \lambda_{k}$ be the distinct eigenvalues of $T$.
    Then

    - (a) $T$ is diagonalizable if and only if the multiplicity of $\lambda_{i}$ is equal to $\operatorname{dim}\left(E_{\lambda_{i}}\right)$ for all $i$.
    - (b) If $T$ is diagonalizable and $\beta_{i}$ is an ordered basis for $E_{\lambda_{i}}$ for each $i$, then $\beta=\beta_{1} \cup \beta_{2} \cup \cdots \cup \beta_{k}$ is an ordered basis for $V$ consisting of eigenvectors of $T$.

    !!! proof
        For each $i$, let $m_{i}$ denote the multiplicity of $\lambda_{i}$, $d_{i}=\operatorname{dim}\left(E_{\lambda_{i}}\right)$, and $n=\operatorname{dim}(V)$.

        First, suppose that $T$ is diagonalizable.
        Let $\beta$ be a basis for $V$ consisting of eigenvectors of $T$.
        For each $i$, let $\beta_{i}=\beta \cap E_{\lambda_{i}}$, the set of vectors in $\beta$ that are eigenvectors corresponding to $\lambda_{i}$, and let $n_{i}$ denote the number of vectors in $\beta_{i}$.
        Then $n_{i} \leq d_{i}$ for each $i$ because $\beta_{i}$ is a linearly independent subset of a subspace of dimension $d_{i}$, and $d_{i} \leq m_{i}$ by **Theorem 24.7**.
        The $n_{i}$ 's sum to $n$ because $\beta$ contains $n$ vectors.
        The $m_{i}$ 's also sum to $n$ because the degree of the characteristic polynomial of $T$ is equal to the sum of the multiplicities of the eigenvalues.
        Thus

        $$
        n=\sum_{i=1}^{k} n_{i} \leq \sum_{i=1}^{k} d_{i} \leq \sum_{i=1}^{k} m_{i}=n
        $$

        It follows that

        $$
        \sum_{i=1}^{k}\left(m_{i}-d_{i}\right)=0
        $$

        Since $\left(m_{i}-d_{i}\right) \geq 0$ for all $i$, we conclude that $m_{i}=d_{i}$ for all $i$.

        Conversely, suppose that $m_{i}=d_{i}$ for all $i$.
        We simultaneously show that $T$ is diagonalizable and prove (b).
        For each $i$, let $\beta_{i}$ be an ordered basis for $E_{\lambda_{i}}$, and let $\beta=\beta_{1} \cup \beta_{2} \cup \cdots \cup \beta_{k}$.
        By **Theorem 24.9**, $\beta$ is linearly independent.
        Furthermore, since $d_{i}=m_{i}$ for all $i$, $\beta$ contains

        $$
        \sum_{i=1}^{k} d_{i}=\sum_{i=1}^{k} m_{i}=n
        $$

        vectors.
        Therefore $\beta$ is an ordered basis for $V$ consisting of eigenvectors of $T$, and we conclude that $T$ is diagonalizable.

## Test for Diagonalization

!!! concept "Concept 24.11 : Test for diagonalization"
    Let $T$ be a linear operator on an $n$-dimensional vector space $V$.
    Then $T$ is diagonalizable if and only if both of the following conditions hold.

    1. The characteristic polynomial of $T$ splits.
    2. For each eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $T$, the multiplicity of $\lambda$ equals $n-\operatorname{rank}(T-\lambda I)$.

    These same conditions can be used to test if a square matrix $A$ is diagonalizable because diagonalizability of $A$ is equivalent to diagonalizability of the operator $L_{A}$.

    If $T$ is a diagonalizable operator and $\beta_{1}, \beta_{2}, \ldots, \beta_{k}$ are ordered bases for the eigenspaces of $T$, then the union $\beta=\beta_{1} \cup \beta_{2} \cup \cdots \cup \beta_{k}$ is an ordered basis for $V$ consisting of eigenvectors of $T$, and hence $[T]_{\beta}$ is a diagonal matrix.

    When testing $T$ for diagonalizability, it is usually easiest to choose a convenient basis $\alpha$ for $V$ and work with $B=[T]_{\alpha}$.
    If the characteristic polynomial of $B$ splits, then use condition 2 above to check if the multiplicity of each repeated eigenvalue of $B$ equals $n-\operatorname{rank}(B-\lambda I)$.
    (By **Theorem 24.7**, condition 2 is automatically satisfied for eigenvalues of multiplicity 1.)
    If so, then $B$, and hence $T$, is diagonalizable.

    If $T$ is diagonalizable and a basis $\beta$ for $V$ consisting of eigenvectors of $T$ is desired, then we first find a basis for each eigenspace of $B$.
    The union of these bases is a basis $\gamma$ for $F^{n}$ consisting of eigenvectors of $B$.
    Each vector in $\gamma$ is the coordinate vector relative to $\alpha$ of an eigenvector of $T$.
    The set consisting of these $n$ eigenvectors of $T$ is the desired basis $\beta$.

    Furthermore, if $A$ is an $n \times n$ diagonalizable matrix, we can use **Corollary 12.9** to find an invertible $n \times n$ matrix $Q$ and a diagonal $n \times n$ matrix $D$ such that $Q^{-1} A Q=D$.
    The matrix $Q$ has as its columns the vectors in a basis of eigenvectors of $A$, and $D$ has as its $j$ th diagonal entry the eigenvalue of $A$ corresponding to the $j$ th column of $Q$.

!!! example "Example 24.12 : Testing a matrix for diagonalizability"
    We test the matrix

    $$
    A=\left(\begin{array}{lll}
    3 & 1 & 0 \\
    0 & 3 & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & 4
    \end{array}\right) \in \mathrm{M}_{3 \times 3}(\mathbb{R})
    $$

    for diagonalizability.

    The characteristic polynomial of $A$ is $\operatorname{det}(A-t I)=-(t-4)(t-3)^{2}$, which splits, and so condition 1 of the test for diagonalization is satisfied.
    Also $A$ has eigenvalues $\lambda_{1}=4$ and $\lambda_{2}=3$ with multiplicities 1 and 2, respectively.
    Since $\lambda_{1}$ has multiplicity 1, condition 2 is satisfied for $\lambda_{1}$.
    Thus we need only test condition 2 for $\lambda_{2}$.
    Because

    $$
    A-\lambda_{2} I=\left(\begin{array}{lll}
    0 & 1 & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & 1
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

    has rank 2, we see that $3-\operatorname{rank}\left(A-\lambda_{2} I\right)=1$, which is not the multiplicity of $\lambda_{2}$.
    Thus condition 2 fails for $\lambda_{2}$, and $A$ is therefore not diagonalizable.

!!! example "Example 24.13 : Testing a linear operator for diagonalizability"
    Let $T$ be the linear operator on $\mathrm{P}_{2}(\mathbb{R})$ defined by

    $$
    T(f(x))=f(1)+f^{\prime}(0) x+\left(f^{\prime}(0)+f^{\prime \prime}(0)\right) x^{2}
    $$

    We first test $T$ for diagonalizability.
    Let $\alpha$ denote the standard ordered basis for $\mathrm{P}_{2}(\mathbb{R})$ and $B=[T]_{\alpha}$.
    Then

    $$
    B=\left(\begin{array}{lll}
    1 & 1 & 1 \\
    0 & 1 & 0 \\
    0 & 1 & 2
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

    The characteristic polynomial of $B$, and hence of $T$, is $-(t-1)^{2}(t-2)$, which splits.
    Hence condition 1 of the test for diagonalization is satisfied.
    Also $B$ has the eigenvalues $\lambda_{1}=1$ and $\lambda_{2}=2$ with multiplicities 2 and 1, respectively.
    Condition 2 is satisfied for $\lambda_{2}$ because it has multiplicity 1.
    So we need only verify condition 2 for $\lambda_{1}=1$.
    For this case,

    $$
    3-\operatorname{rank}\left(B-\lambda_{1} I\right)=3-\operatorname{rank}\left(\begin{array}{lll}
    0 & 1 & 1 \\
    0 & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & 1 & 1
    \end{array}\right)=3-1=2
    $$

    which is equal to the multiplicity of $\lambda_{1}$.
    Therefore $T$ is diagonalizable.
    We now find an ordered basis $\gamma$ for $\mathbb{R}^{3}$ of eigenvectors of $B$.
    We consider each eigenvalue separately.

    The eigenspace corresponding to $\lambda_{1}=1$ is

    $$
    E_{\lambda_{1}}=\left\{\left(\begin{array}{l}
    x_{1} \\
    x_{2} \\
    x_{3}
    \end{array}\right) \in \mathbb{R}^{3}:\left(\begin{array}{lll}
    0 & 1 & 1 \\
    0 & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & 1 & 1
    \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{l}
    x_{1} \\
    x_{2} \\
    x_{3}
    \end{array}\right)=0\right\}
    $$

    which is the solution space for the system

    $$
    x_{2}+x_{3}=0
    $$

    and has

    $$
    \gamma_{1}=\left\{\left(\begin{array}{l}
    1 \\
    0 \\
    0
    \end{array}\right),\left(\begin{array}{r}
    0 \\
    -1 \\
    1
    \end{array}\right)\right\}
    $$

    as a basis.
    The eigenspace corresponding to $\lambda_{2}=2$ is

    $$
    E_{\lambda_{2}}=\left\{\left(\begin{array}{l}
    x_{1} \\
    x_{2} \\
    x_{3}
    \end{array}\right) \in \mathbb{R}^{3}:\left(\begin{array}{rrr}
    -1 & 1 & 1 \\
    0 & -1 & 0 \\
    0 & 1 & 0
    \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{l}
    x_{1} \\
    x_{2} \\
    x_{3}
    \end{array}\right)=0\right\}
    $$

    which is the solution space for the system

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    -x_{1}+x_{2}+x_{3} & =0 \\
    x_{2} & =0,
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    and has

    $$
    \gamma_{2}=\left\{\left(\begin{array}{l}
    1 \\
    0 \\
    1
    \end{array}\right)\right\}
    $$

    as a basis.
    Let

    $$
    \gamma=\gamma_{1} \cup \gamma_{2}=\left\{\left(\begin{array}{l}
    1 \\
    0 \\
    0
    \end{array}\right),\left(\begin{array}{r}
    0 \\
    -1 \\
    1
    \end{array}\right),\left(\begin{array}{l}
    1 \\
    0 \\
    1
    \end{array}\right)\right\} .
    $$

    Then $\gamma$ is an ordered basis for $\mathbb{R}^{3}$ consisting of eigenvectors of $B$.
    Finally, observe that the vectors in $\gamma$ are the coordinate vectors relative to $\alpha$ of the vectors in the set

    $$
    \beta=\left\{1,-x+x^{2}, 1+x^{2}\right\}
    $$

    which is an ordered basis for $\mathrm{P}_{2}(\mathbb{R})$ consisting of eigenvectors of $T$.
    Thus

    $$
    [T]_{\beta}=\left(\begin{array}{lll}
    1 & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & 1 & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & 2
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

!!! example "Example 24.14 : Computing the power of a matrix using diagonalization"
    Let

    $$
    A=\left(\begin{array}{rr}
    0 & -2 \\
    1 & 3
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

    We show that $A$ is diagonalizable and find a $2 \times 2$ matrix $Q$ such that $Q^{-1} A Q$ is a diagonal matrix.
    We then show how to use this result to compute $A^{n}$ for any positive integer $n$.

    First observe that the characteristic polynomial of $A$ is $(t-1)(t-2)$, and hence $A$ has two distinct eigenvalues, $\lambda_{1}=1$ and $\lambda_{2}=2$.
    By applying the corollary to **Theorem 24.1** to the operator $L_{A}$, we see that $A$ is diagonalizable.
    Moreover,

    $$
    \gamma_{1}=\left\{\binom{-2}{1}\right\} \quad \text { and } \quad \gamma_{2}=\left\{\binom{-1}{1}\right\}
    $$

    are bases for the eigenspaces $E_{\lambda_{1}}$ and $E_{\lambda_{2}}$, respectively.
    Therefore

    $$
    \gamma=\gamma_{1} \cup \gamma_{2}=\left\{\binom{-2}{1},\binom{-1}{1}\right\}
    $$

    is an ordered basis for $\mathbb{R}^{2}$ consisting of eigenvectors of $A$.
    Let

    $$
    Q=\left(\begin{array}{rr}
    -2 & -1 \\
    1 & 1
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

    the matrix whose columns are the vectors in $\gamma$.
    Then, by the corollary to **Theorem 12.6**,

    $$
    D=Q^{-1} A Q=\left[L_{A}\right]_{\beta}=\left(\begin{array}{ll}
    1 & 0 \\
    0 & 2
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

    To find $A^{n}$ for any positive integer $n$, observe that $A=Q D Q^{-1}$.
    Therefore

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    A^{n} & =\left(Q D Q^{-1}\right)^{n} \\
    & =\left(Q D Q^{-1}\right)\left(Q D Q^{-1}\right) \cdots\left(Q D Q^{-1}\right) \\
    & =Q D^{n} Q^{-1} \\
    & =Q\left(\begin{array}{rr}
    1^{n} & 0 \\
    0 & 2^{n}
    \end{array}\right) Q^{-1} \\
    & =\left(\begin{array}{rr}
    -2 & -1 \\
    1 & 1
    \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{rr}
    1 & 0 \\
    0 & 2^{n}
    \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{rr}
    -1 & -1 \\
    1 & 2
    \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{rr}
    2-2^{n} & 2-2^{n+1} \\
    -1+2^{n} & -1+2^{n+1}
    \end{array}\right) .
    \end{aligned}
    $$

## Direct Sums

!!! definition "Definition 24.15 : Sum of Subspaces"
    Let $W_{1}, W_{2}, \ldots, W_{k}$ be subspaces of a vector space $V$.
    We define the **sum** of these subspaces to be the set

    $$
    \left\{v_{1}+v_{2}+\cdots+v_{k}: v_{i} \in W_{i} \text { for } 1 \leq i \leq k\right\}
    $$

    which we denote by $W_{1}+W_{2}+\cdots+W_{k}$ or $\sum_{i=1}^{k} W_{i}$.

!!! definition "Definition 24.16 : Direct Sum of Subspaces"
    Let $W_{1}, W_{2}, \ldots, W_{k}$ be subspaces of a vector space $V$.
    We call $V$ the **direct sum** of the subspaces $W_{1}, W_{2}, \ldots, W_{k}$ and write $V=W_{1} \oplus W_{2} \oplus \cdots \oplus W_{k}$, if

    $$
    V=\sum_{i=1}^{k} W_{i}
    $$

    and

    $$
    W_{j} \cap \sum_{i \neq j} W_{i}=\{0\} \quad \text { for each } j(1 \leq j \leq k)
    $$

!!! theorem "Theorem 24.17 : Equivalent conditions to a direct sum"
    Let $W_{1}, W_{2}, \ldots, W_{k}$ be subspaces of a finite-dimensional vector space $V$.
    The following conditions are equivalent.

    - (a) $V=W_{1} \oplus W_{2} \oplus \cdots \oplus W_{k}$.
    - (b) $V=\sum_{i=1}^{k} W_{i}$ and, for any vectors $v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots, v_{k}$ such that $v_{i} \in W_{i}$ $(1 \leq i \leq k)$, if $v_{1}+v_{2}+\cdots+v_{k}=0$, then $v_{i}=0$ for all $i$.
    - (c) Each vector $v \in V$ can be uniquely written as $v=v_{1}+v_{2}+\cdots+v_{k}$, where $v_{i} \in W_{i}$.
    - (d) If $\gamma_{i}$ is an ordered basis for $W_{i}$ $(1 \leq i \leq k)$, then $\gamma_{1} \cup \gamma_{2} \cup \cdots \cup \gamma_{k}$ is an ordered basis for $V$.
    - (e) For each $i=1,2, \ldots, k$, there exists an ordered basis $\gamma_{i}$ for $W_{i}$ such that $\gamma_{1} \cup \gamma_{2} \cup \cdots \cup \gamma_{k}$ is an ordered basis for $V$.

    !!! proof
        Assume (a).
        We prove (b).
        Clearly

        $$
        V=\sum_{i=1}^{k} W_{i}
        $$

        Now suppose that $v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots, v_{k}$ are vectors such that $v_{i} \in W_{i}$ for all $i$ and $v_{1}+v_{2}+\cdots+v_{k}=0$.
        Then for any $j$

        $$
        -v_{j}=\sum_{i \neq j} v_{i} \in \sum_{i \neq j} W_{i}
        $$

        But $-v_{j} \in W_{j}$ and hence

        $$
        -v_{j} \in W_{j} \cap \sum_{i \neq j} W_{i}=\{0\}
        $$

        So $v_{j}=0$, proving (b).
        Now assume (b).
        We prove (c).
        Let $v \in V$.
        By (b), there exist vectors $v_{1}, v_{2}, \ldots, v_{k}$ such that $v_{i} \in W_{i}$ and $v=v_{1}+v_{2}+\cdots+v_{k}$.
        We must show that this representation is unique.
        Suppose also that $v=w_{1}+w_{2}+\cdots+w_{k}$, where $w_{i} \in W_{i}$ for all $i$.
        Then

        $$
        \left(v_{1}-w_{1}\right)+\left(v_{2}-w_{2}\right)+\cdots+\left(v_{k}-w_{k}\right)=0
        $$

        But $v_{i}-w_{i} \in W_{i}$ for all $i$, and therefore $v_{i}-w_{i}=0$ for all $i$ by (b).
        Thus $v_{i}=w_{i}$ for all $i$, proving the uniqueness of the representation.

        Now assume (c).
        We prove (d).
        For each $i$, let $\gamma_{i}$ be an ordered basis for $W_{i}$.
        Since

        $$
        V=\sum_{i=1}^{k} W_{i}
        $$

        by (c), it follows that $\gamma_{1} \cup \gamma_{2} \cup \cdots \cup \gamma_{k}$ generates $V$.
        To show that this set is linearly independent, consider vectors $v_{i j} \in \gamma_{i}$ $(j=1,2, \ldots, m_{i}$ and $i=1,2, \ldots, k)$ and scalars $a_{i j}$ such that

        $$
        \sum_{i, j} a_{i j} v_{i j}=0
        $$

        For each $i$, set

        $$
        w_{i}=\sum_{j=1}^{m_{i}} a_{i j} v_{i j} .
        $$

        Then for each $i$, $w_{i} \in \operatorname{span}\left(\gamma_{i}\right)=W_{i}$ and

        $$
        w_{1}+w_{2}+\cdots+w_{k}=\sum_{i, j} a_{i j} v_{i j}=0
        $$

        Since $0 \in W_{i}$ for each $i$ and $0+0+\cdots+0=w_{1}+w_{2}+\cdots+w_{k}$, (c) implies that $w_{i}=0$ for all $i$.
        Thus

        $$
        0=w_{i}=\sum_{j=1}^{m_{i}} a_{i j} v_{i j}
        $$

        for each $i$.
        But each $\gamma_{i}$ is linearly independent, and hence $a_{i j}=0$ for all $i$ and $j$.
        Consequently $\gamma_{1} \cup \gamma_{2} \cup \cdots \cup \gamma_{k}$ is linearly independent and therefore is a basis for $V$.

        Clearly (e) follows immediately from (d).
        Finally, we assume (e) and prove (a).
        For each $i$, let $\gamma_{i}$ be an ordered basis for $W_{i}$ such that $\gamma_{1} \cup \gamma_{2} \cup \cdots \cup \gamma_{k}$ is an ordered basis for $V$.
        Then

        $$
        V=\operatorname{span}\left(\gamma_{1} \cup \gamma_{2} \cup \cdots \cup \gamma_{k}\right)
        $$

        $$
        =\operatorname{span}\left(\gamma_{1}\right)+\operatorname{span}\left(\gamma_{2}\right)+\cdots+\operatorname{span}\left(\gamma_{k}\right)=\sum_{i=1}^{k} W_{i}
        $$

        by repeated applications of **Exercise 4.14**.
        Fix $j(1 \leq j \leq k)$, and suppose that, for some nonzero vector $v \in V$,

        $$
        v \in W_{j} \cap \sum_{i \neq j} W_{i}
        $$

        Then

        $$
        v \in W_{j}=\operatorname{span}\left(\gamma_{j}\right) \quad \text { and } \quad v \in \sum_{i \neq j} W_{i}=\operatorname{span}\left(\bigcup_{i \neq j} \gamma_{i}\right) .
        $$

        Hence $v$ is a nontrivial linear combination of both $\gamma_{j}$ and $\left(\bigcup_{i \neq j} \gamma_{i}\right)$, so that $v$ can be expressed as a linear combination of $\gamma_{1} \cup \gamma_{2} \cup \cdots \cup \gamma_{k}$ in more than one way.
        But these representations contradict **Theorem 6.2**, and so we conclude that

        $$
        W_{j} \cap \sum_{i \neq j} W_{i}=\{0\}
        $$

        proving (a).

!!! theorem "Theorem 24.18 : A linear operator is diagonalizable iff the space is a direct sum of eigenspaces."
    A linear operator $T$ on a finite-dimensional vector space $V$ is diagonalizable if and only if $V$ is the direct sum of the eigenspaces of $T$.

    !!! proof
        Let $\lambda_{1}, \lambda_{2}, \ldots, \lambda_{k}$ be the distinct eigenvalues of $T$.
        First suppose that $T$ is diagonalizable, and for each $i$ choose an ordered basis $\gamma_{i}$ for the eigenspace $E_{\lambda_{i}}$.
        By **Theorem 24.10**, $\gamma_{1} \cup \gamma_{2} \cup \cdots \cup \gamma_{k}$ is a basis for $V$, and hence $V$ is a direct sum of the $E_{\lambda_{i}}$ 's by **Theorem 24.17**.

        Conversely, suppose that $V$ is a direct sum of the eigenspaces of $T$.
        For each $i$, choose an ordered basis $\gamma_{i}$ of $E_{\lambda_{i}}$.
        By **Theorem 24.17**, the union $\gamma_{1} \cup \gamma_{2} \cup \cdots \cup \gamma_{k}$ is a basis for $V$.
        Since this basis consists of eigenvectors of $T$, we conclude that $T$ is diagonalizable.

## Application : Systems of Differential Equations

!!! example "Example 24.19 : Solving a system of differential equations by diagonalization"
    Consider the system of differential equations

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    x_{1}^{\prime} & =3 x_{1}+x_{2}+x_{3} \\
    x_{2}^{\prime} & =2 x_{1}+4 x_{2}+2 x_{3} \\
    x_{3}^{\prime} & =-x_{1}-x_{2}+x_{3},
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    where, for each $i, x_{i}=x_{i}(t)$ is a differentiable real-valued function of the real variable $t$.
    Clearly, this system has a solution, namely, the solution in which each $x_{i}(t)$ is the zero function.
    We determine all of the solutions to this system.

    Let $x: \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{3}$ be the function defined by

    $$
    x(t)=\left(\begin{array}{l}
    x_{1}(t) \\
    x_{2}(t) \\
    x_{3}(t)
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

    The derivative of $x$, denoted $x^{\prime}$, is defined by

    $$
    x^{\prime}(t)=\left(\begin{array}{c}
    x_{1}^{\prime}(t) \\
    x_{2}^{\prime}(t) \\
    x_{3}^{\prime}(t)
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

    Let

    $$
    A=\left(\begin{array}{rrr}
    3 & 1 & 1 \\
    2 & 4 & 2 \\
    -1 & -1 & 1
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

    be the coefficient matrix of the given system, so that we can rewrite the system as the matrix equation $x^{\prime}=A x$.

    It can be verified that for

    $$
    Q=\left(\begin{array}{rrr}
    -1 & 0 & -1 \\
    0 & -1 & -2 \\
    1 & 1 & 1
    \end{array}\right) \quad \text { and } \quad D=\left(\begin{array}{lll}
    2 & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & 2 & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & 4
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

    we have $Q^{-1} A Q=D$.
    Substitute $A=Q D Q^{-1}$ into $x^{\prime}=A x$ to obtain $x^{\prime}=Q D Q^{-1} x$ or, equivalently, $Q^{-1} x^{\prime}=D Q^{-1} x$.
    The function $y: \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{3}$ defined by $y(t)=Q^{-1} x(t)$ can be shown to be differentiable (see **Exercise 24.16**), and $y^{\prime}=Q^{-1} x^{\prime}$.
    Hence the original system can be written as $y^{\prime}=D y$.

    Since $D$ is a diagonal matrix, the system $y^{\prime}=D y$ is easy to solve.
    Setting

    $$
    y(t)=\left(\begin{array}{l}
    y_{1}(t) \\
    y_{2}(t) \\
    y_{3}(t)
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

    we can rewrite $y^{\prime}=D y$ as

    $$
    \left(\begin{array}{l}
    y_{1}^{\prime}(t) \\
    y_{2}^{\prime}(t) \\
    y_{3}^{\prime}(t)
    \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{lll}
    2 & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & 2 & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & 4
    \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{l}
    y_{1}(t) \\
    y_{2}(t) \\
    y_{3}(t)
    \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l}
    2 y_{1}(t) \\
    2 y_{2}(t) \\
    4 y_{3}(t)
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

    The three equations

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    & y_{1}^{\prime}=2 y_{1} \\
    & y_{2}^{\prime}=2 y_{2} \\
    & y_{3}^{\prime}=4 y_{3}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    are independent of each other, and thus can be solved individually.
    It is easily seen that the general solution to these equations is $y_{1}(t)=c_{1} e^{2 t}, y_{2}(t)=c_{2} e^{2 t}$, and $y_{3}(t)=c_{3} e^{4 t}$, where $c_{1}, c_{2}$, and $c_{3}$ are arbitrary constants.
    Finally,

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \left(\begin{array}{l}
    x_{1}(t) \\
    x_{2}(t) \\
    x_{3}(t)
    \end{array}\right) & =x(t)=Q y(t)=\left(\begin{array}{rrr}
    -1 & 0 & -1 \\
    0 & -1 & -2 \\
    1 & 1 & 1
    \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{l}
    c_{1} e^{2 t} \\
    c_{2} e^{2 t} \\
    c_{3} e^{4 t}
    \end{array}\right) \\
    & =\binom{-c_{1} e^{2 t}-c_{2} e^{2 t}-2 c_{3} e^{4 t} e^{4 t}}{c_{1} e^{2 t}+c_{2} e^{2 t}+c_{3} e^{4 t}}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    yields the general solution of the original system.
    Note that this solution can be written as

    $$
    x(t)=e^{2 t}\left[c_{1}\left(\begin{array}{r}
    -1 \\
    0 \\
    1
    \end{array}\right)+c_{2}\left(\begin{array}{r}
    0 \\
    -1 \\
    1
    \end{array}\right)\right]+e^{4 t}\left[c_{3}\left(\begin{array}{r}
    -1 \\
    -2 \\
    1
    \end{array}\right)\right]
    $$

    The expressions in brackets are arbitrary vectors in $E_{\lambda_{1}}$ and $E_{\lambda_{2}}$, respectively, where $\lambda_{1}=2$ and $\lambda_{2}=4$.
    Thus the general solution of the original system is $x(t)=e^{2 t} z_{1}+e^{4 t} z_{2}$, where $z_{1} \in E_{\lambda_{1}}$ and $z_{2} \in E_{\lambda_{2}}$.


!!! theorem "Theorem 24.20 : Generalization of solving a system of differential equations by diagonalization"
    Let

    $$
    A=\left(\begin{array}{cccc}
    a_{11} & a_{12} & \cdots & a_{1 n} \\
    a_{21} & a_{22} & \cdots & a_{2 n} \\
    \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\
    a_{n 1} & a_{n 2} & \cdots & a_{n n}
    \end{array}\right)
    $$

    be the coefficient matrix of the system of differential equations

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    x_{1}^{\prime} & =a_{11} x_{1}+a_{12} x_{2}+\cdots+a_{1 n} x_{n} \\
    x_{2}^{\prime} & =a_{21} x_{1}+a_{22} x_{2}+\cdots+a_{2 n} x_{n} \\
    \vdots & \\
    x_{n}^{\prime} & =a_{n 1} x_{1}+a_{n 2} x_{2}+\cdots+a_{n n} x_{n} .
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    Suppose that $A$ is diagonalizable and that the distinct eigenvalues of $A$ are $\lambda_{1}, \lambda_{2}, \ldots, \lambda_{k}$.
    Then a differentiable function $x: \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{n}$ is a solution to the system if and only if $x$ is of the form

    $$
    x(t)=e^{\lambda_{1} t} z_{1}+e^{\lambda_{2} t} z_{2}+\cdots+e^{\lambda_{k} t} z_{k}
    $$

    where $z_{i} \in E_{\lambda_{i}}$ for $i=1,2, \ldots, k$.
    Moreover, the set of solutions to the system is an $n$-dimensional real vector space.

    !!! proof
        Let $V=\mathbb{R}^{n}$ and view $A$ as the matrix of the linear operator $T=L_{A}:V\rightarrow V$.
        The system can be written compactly as

        $$
        x^{\prime}(t)=A x(t).
        $$

        - (**Only if**)  
            Assume $x:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}^{n}$ is a differentiable solution of $x^{\prime}=Ax$.
            Since $A$ is diagonalizable and has distinct eigenvalues $\lambda_{1},\ldots,\lambda_{k}$, we have a direct-sum decomposition

            $$
            \mathbb{R}^{n}=E_{\lambda_{1}}\oplus E_{\lambda_{2}}\oplus\cdots\oplus E_{\lambda_{k}} .
            $$

            Therefore, for each $t$, there exist unique vectors $x_{i}(t)\in E_{\lambda_{i}}$ such that

            $$
            x(t)=x_{1}(t)+x_{2}(t)+\cdots+x_{k}(t).
            $$

            Let $P_{i}:\mathbb{R}^{n}\to E_{\lambda_{i}}$ denote the projection onto $E_{\lambda_{i}}$ along the direct sum of the other eigenspaces, so that $x_{i}(t)=P_{i}(x(t))$.
            Because $P_{i}$ is linear, we have

            $$
            x_{i}^{\prime}(t)=(P_{i}x)^{\prime}(t)=P_{i}(x^{\prime}(t))=P_{i}(A x(t)).
            $$

            Also, each $P_{i}$ commutes with $A$.
            To see this, let $v=v_{1}+\cdots+v_{k}$ with $v_{j}\in E_{\lambda_{j}}$.
            Then

            $$
            A(P_{i}v)=A(v_{i})=\lambda_{i}v_{i}
            $$

            and

            $$
            P_{i}(Av)=P_{i}\!\left(\sum_{j=1}^{k}A(v_{j})\right)=P_{i}\!\left(\sum_{j=1}^{k}\lambda_{j}v_{j}\right)=\lambda_{i}v_{i}.
            $$

            Hence $P_{i}A=AP_{i}$, and therefore

            $$
            x_{i}^{\prime}(t)=P_{i}(A x(t))=A(P_{i}x(t))=A x_{i}(t).
            $$

            But $x_{i}(t)\in E_{\lambda_{i}}$, so $A x_{i}(t)=\lambda_{i}x_{i}(t)$.
            Thus

            $$
            x_{i}^{\prime}(t)=\lambda_{i}x_{i}(t).
            $$

            Define $z_{i}(t)=e^{-\lambda_{i}t}x_{i}(t)$.
            Then

            $$
            z_{i}^{\prime}(t)= -\lambda_{i}e^{-\lambda_{i}t}x_{i}(t)+e^{-\lambda_{i}t}x_{i}^{\prime}(t)
            =e^{-\lambda_{i}t}\bigl(x_{i}^{\prime}(t)-\lambda_{i}x_{i}(t)\bigr)=0.
            $$

            Hence each $z_{i}(t)$ is constant, say $z_{i}(t)\equiv z_{i}$, and $z_{i}\in E_{\lambda_{i}}$ because $x_{i}(t)\in E_{\lambda_{i}}$ for all $t$.
            Therefore $x_{i}(t)=e^{\lambda_{i}t}z_{i}$ and

            $$
            x(t)=\sum_{i=1}^{k}x_{i}(t)=\sum_{i=1}^{k}e^{\lambda_{i}t}z_{i}.
            $$

        - (**If**)  
            Conversely, suppose

            $$
            x(t)=\sum_{i=1}^{k}e^{\lambda_{i}t}z_{i}
            $$

            where each $z_{i}\in E_{\lambda_{i}}$.
            Then $A z_{i}=\lambda_{i}z_{i}$, and differentiating term-by-term gives

            $$
            x^{\prime}(t)=\sum_{i=1}^{k}\lambda_{i}e^{\lambda_{i}t}z_{i}
            =\sum_{i=1}^{k}e^{\lambda_{i}t}(\lambda_{i}z_{i})
            =\sum_{i=1}^{k}e^{\lambda_{i}t}(A z_{i})
            =A\sum_{i=1}^{k}e^{\lambda_{i}t}z_{i}
            =A x(t).
            $$

            Hence $x$ is a solution.

        - (**Vector space and dimension**)  
            Let $\mathcal{S}$ denote the set of all solutions $x:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}^{n}$ of $x^{\prime}=Ax$.
            If $x,y\in\mathcal{S}$ and $c\in\mathbb{R}$, then $(x+y)^{\prime}=x^{\prime}+y^{\prime}=Ax+Ay=A(x+y)$ and $(c x)^{\prime}=c x^{\prime}=cAx=A(cx)$, so $\mathcal{S}$ is a real vector space.

            Define a map $\Psi:\mathcal{S}\to\mathbb{R}^{n}$ by $\Psi(x)=x(0)$.
            This map is linear.
            By the representation proved above, for any solution $x(t)=\sum_{i=1}^{k}e^{\lambda_{i}t}z_{i}$ we have

            $$
            \Psi(x)=x(0)=\sum_{i=1}^{k}z_{i}.
            $$

            Because $\mathbb{R}^{n}=E_{\lambda_{1}}\oplus\cdots\oplus E_{\lambda_{k}}$, every vector $v\in\mathbb{R}^{n}$ can be written uniquely as $v=\sum_{i=1}^{k}z_{i}$ with $z_{i}\in E_{\lambda_{i}}$.
            For such $v$, define

            $$
            x_{v}(t)=\sum_{i=1}^{k}e^{\lambda_{i}t}z_{i}.
            $$

            Then $x_{v}\in\mathcal{S}$ and $\Psi(x_{v})=v$, so $\Psi$ is onto.
            Also, if $\Psi(x)=0$, then $x(0)=0$, so $\sum_{i=1}^{k}z_{i}=0$, which implies each $z_{i}=0$ by uniqueness of the direct-sum decomposition, hence $x(t)\equiv 0$; thus $\Psi$ is one-to-one.
            Therefore $\Psi$ is an isomorphism, and

            $$
            \operatorname{dim}(\mathcal{S})=\operatorname{dim}(\mathbb{R}^{n})=n.
            $$

!!! definition "Definition 24.21 : Simultaneous Diagonalizability"
    Two linear operators $T$ and $U$ on a finite-dimensional vector space $V$ are called **simultaneously diagonalizable** if there exists an ordered basis $\beta$ for $V$ such that both $[T]_{\beta}$ and $[\mathrm{U}]_{\beta}$ are diagonal matrices.
    
    Similarly, $A, B \in \mathrm{M}_{n \times n}(F)$ are called **simultaneously diagonalizable** if there exists an invertible matrix $Q \in \mathrm{M}_{n \times n}(F)$ such that both $Q^{-1} A Q$ and $Q^{-1} B Q$ are diagonal matrices.
